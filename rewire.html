The Easyway Method
Most methods treat smoking as a pleasure you're sacrificing — creating deprivation that drives you back. Allen Carr's Easyway works differently: it dissolves the illusions that make you think smoking does something for you. When those illusions are gone, the desire to smoke simply disappears. No willpower. No white-knuckling. No substitutes.
Smoking merely relieves the withdrawal caused by the previous cigarette. Every cigarette creates the very problem it appears to solve. When you truly understand this, the desire to smoke disappears — you are not giving anything up.
Allen Carr's Easyway principle
While you read this section

The illusions — tap each to reveal the truth
"Smoking relaxes me."
The Relaxation Illusion
▾
Nicotine is a stimulant, not a relaxant. What feels like relaxation is the relief of the tension and anxiety that nicotine itself caused. A non-smoker is already in the calm state you're trying to reach. When you smoke you temporarily return to baseline — then the drug wears off and the anxiety builds again until the next cigarette. The cigarette created the very problem it appears to solve.
"I enjoy it. It's a genuine pleasure."
The Pleasure Illusion
▾
Your first cigarette tasted awful. The "pleasure" you feel now is entirely manufactured by the addiction — it's the brief relief of silencing the craving the addiction created. Like wearing tight shoes all day for the pleasure of taking them off. You're not experiencing pleasure; you're experiencing relief from a discomfort you chose to create. Remove the addiction, and the "pleasure" has nothing to stand on.
"It helps me concentrate / think."
The Focus Illusion
▾
Nicotine withdrawal causes restlessness and foggy thinking. A cigarette relieves that withdrawal, restoring the mental clarity you had before the last cigarette wore off. You're not enhancing your concentration — you're restoring it to the level a non-smoker maintains continuously, without any chemical aid at all.
"Quitting will make me miserable / anxious."
The Withdrawal Fear Illusion
▾
The physical withdrawal from nicotine is genuinely mild — a faint, slightly uncomfortable feeling that fades within days. What makes quitting feel unbearable is the mental belief that you're sacrificing something precious. Once you understand there is nothing real being given up — because cigarettes only provided what a non-smoker has naturally — the mental battle evaporates. You're not losing a crutch. You're removing a trap.
"I'll gain weight when I quit."
The Weight Illusion
▾
Weight gain happens when people substitute food for cigarettes — eating to fill a void they believe quitting created. If you quit without feeling deprived, there is no void to fill. Nicotine raises metabolism only marginally — look at the number of overweight smokers. Once free, your sense of taste improves, food becomes more satisfying in smaller amounts, and your capacity for exercise increases significantly. The fear of weight gain is itself part of the trap keeping you smoking.
"Smoking is my stress relief."
The Stress Relief Illusion
▾
Research consistently shows smokers have higher baseline anxiety than non-smokers. The "stress" that smoking relieves is largely nicotine withdrawal compounding ordinary life stress. Smoking creates a loop: stress → smoke → brief relief → more withdrawal anxiety → more stress → smoke again. Non-smokers face identical life pressures without needing a cigarette. Once free, you'll have one fewer source of anxiety — not one fewer tool to manage it.
"I'm just an addictive person."
The Identity Illusion
▾
Nicotine is one of the most physically addictive substances known. The vast majority of people who try it regularly become dependent. This says nothing about your character — it says everything about the drug. The neural pathways of addiction were built by the drug, not by a flaw in you. And just as they were built by repetition, they can be unmade through neuroplasticity. You are not an addict. You are a person who walked into a chemical trap that catches almost everyone.
"I can't imagine socialising without it."
The Social Prop Illusion
▾
You don't need a cigarette to socialise — billions of people do so without ever smoking. The discomfort of not smoking in a social situation is the discomfort of withdrawal, not a genuine social need. As a non-smoker, you won't have that withdrawal anxiety to begin with. Social situations will actually become more enjoyable — because you won't be distracted by planning your next cigarette or needing to step away from the group.

Your Brain on Nicotine
The Easyway dismantles the psychological trap. Neuroscience explains the biological one. Understanding both makes quitting feel logical rather than heroic.
The Dopamine Hijack
Nicotine floods the nucleus accumbens with dopamine via the ventral tegmental area. Over time the brain downregulates its own receptors — you need nicotine just to feel normal. Every "good" feeling from smoking is simply restoring what the addiction depleted.
Neuroplasticity: Hebb's Law
Neurons that fire together wire together. The smoking habit is a neural highway built by repetition. It can be unpaved the same way — by not using it. Every resisted craving prunes this pathway and every new behaviour in its place strengthens an alternative.
The 90-Second Wave
Dr. Jill Bolte Taylor found that the neurochemical loop of an emotional impulse — including cravings — lasts just 90 seconds if you don't feed it. Every surf completes the loop and weakens the signal.
Receptor Normalization
Your brain grew extra nicotinic receptors in response to nicotine. They begin downregulating within 24 hours of quitting and are largely normalized in 6–12 weeks. After this, the biological craving mechanism is structurally deactivated.
The Insula Cortex
The insula translates emotional states into bodily cravings. Patients with insula damage simply forget to want cigarettes. Mindfulness practice directly trains this region — with repetition, the craving signal weakens and eventually stops generating.
Exercise & BDNF
Physical exercise releases Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor, literally growing new neurons. Even 10 minutes of aerobic movement reduces cravings by up to 40% and builds the new reward circuitry that replaces nicotine dependence.
Sleep & Synaptic Pruning
Deep sleep is when your brain actively prunes weak connections — including the smoking circuit — and consolidates new strong ones. Every good night is active neurological rewiring.

About this app
Rewire combines two approaches. The Easyway (Allen Carr's method) dissolves the psychological illusions that make you want to smoke — no willpower, no NRT, no substitutes. Neuroplasticity science explains the biology of addiction and gives you tools to physically rewire the craving circuits. Together they address both the mental trap and the neural pathway.
